CHAPTER ONE
INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of Study 
Plant diseases have continued to pose a great danger to crop productivity, especially in agriculture which is a crucial component in world food security. Solanaceous plants like tomato, potato, pepper, and eggplant are very susceptible to fungal, bacterial, and viral diseases, which are manifested in the leaves of the plants. Without being identified in their initial stages, these diseases may cause serious losses in yield and financial difficulties to the agricultural societies (Demilie, 2024; Ferentinos, 2018).
Conventionally, the diagnosis of plant diseases relied on manual examination by farmers and agriculturalists. It is a subjective method, time consuming and highly dependent on the experience which is usually lacking in rural farms. These constraints provide a great incentive toward the creation of automatic, scalable, and intelligent solutions that can effectively and precisely detect plant diseases (Joseph et al., 2022; Boulent et al., 2019).
The development of artificial intelligence and computer vision has changed the way farmers solve their problems. Deep learning, especially Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), have proven to be exceptional when it comes to image classification tasks since it is able to automatically consider hierarchical visual features of raw images (Ferentinos, 2018; Lu et al., 2021).
The use of CNNs in plant disease detection has been largely implemented due to the availability of visual symptoms of plant diseases discoloration, lesions, and textural changes. Research has demonstrated that CNN-based methods are superior to use of traditional machine learning methods which use handcrafted features. Also, transfer learning has enhanced the classification accuracy and minimized training time and compute cost (Abade et al., 2020; Hassan et al., 2021; Shafik et al., 2024).
Recent studies have shifted to the real-time plant disease detection systems, in which CNN models are applied to images taken on the camera or embedded devices. These systems allow the use of immediate feedback in the realistic agricultural setting but introduce issues regarding the speed of inference, memory efficiency, and optimization of the system (Gajjar et al., 2021; Khalid et al., 2023).
However, even despite some significant advancements, much of the current literature is devoted to controlled data sets or broad-based crop types, and very little attention is paid to the idea of disease detection in solanaceous crops in real-time. The gap illustrates the necessity of CNN-based systems that would be accurate and efficient in terms of computations in the conditions of the real field (Pandian et al., 2022; Vasconez et al., 2024).
Therefore, this paper will combine computer science, deep learning, and smart farming to create an efficient solanaceous crop plant disease detection system based on CNN architectures in real-time to help in supporting intelligent and practical agricultural decision-making (Jung et al., 2023; Singla et al., 2024).
1.2 Statement of the Problem 
Plant diseases have continued to be the most significant problem that has impacted agricultural production especially when it comes to the growing of solanaceous crops like tomato, potato, pepper and eggplant. The crops are also very susceptible to numerous leaf disorders that may spread very fast and result in massive loss of yield unless they are detected at an early age. These diseases therefore need to be identified in time and correctly to ensure proper management of the diseases and sustainable agriculture.
But today, disease identification methods are mostly manual and rely on the visual inspection of the farmers or the agricultural specialists. This method is also intrinsically constrained by human subjectivity, lack of consistency and the necessity of professional knowledge. Accessibility to plant pathology experts is limited in most of the developing regions such as rural farming communities where it then results in a late or mis diagnosis. In terms of computer science, it shows deficiency of systematically scalable systems that can deliver reliable and timely disease detection.
Even though a few machine learning and deep learning methods have been suggested in the classification of plant diseases, there are a significant number of existing systems that run in offline or laboratory-controlled conditions. They usually use the fixed datasets of images, and they fail to consider any real-time deployment-related issues, like the speed of inference, the computational efficiency, and system responsiveness (Lu et al., 2021; Tugrul et al., 2022). This leaves their application in the real world in an agricultural environment limited.
Besides, although Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have been shown to be accurate in the classification of plant leaf diseases, much of the research has been conducted where general crop datasets are examined and not necessarily solanaceous plants and the limitation of real-time detection (Ferentinos, 2018; Abade et al., 2020). This leaves the gap in the research in the design of CNN-based systems that are crop-specific and optimized in terms of real time operations.
Thus, the main issue of the research is that there is no efficient, real-time, and automatic system of detecting diseases in solanaceous plant leaves using CNN. To solve this issue, there should be the combination of computer vision, deep learning, and real-time system design to provide an accurate, fast, and applicable solution to this practical agricultural implementation.

This feature enables not only the quick procedure of the interventions but also allows using resources more effectively by decreasing the waste of pesticides and fertilizers. 1.7 Limitation of the Study 
Although this study is expected to design an efficient and real-time plant disease detection system of solanaceous crops, there are various limitations that could be found to be inherent as a result of practical, computational, and methodological constraints. First, the system is dedicated to the symptoms of the disease in leaves only. In spite of the fact that leaves tend to show the most evident indications of infection, stems, fruits, and roots are also subject to diseases. Omission of these parts can restrict the overall coverage of the detection of the system, and the disease that can be initially developed in other organs of the plant can remain undetected.
The other limitation is associated with the quality of images and the environment. The CNN model relies on the quality of input images which determine the accuracy of the model. Changes in lighting, background richness, camera density and orientation of the leaves may negatively influence model performance. Although these problems can be reduced with the help of preprocessing steps like normalization, resizing, and data augmentation, the system may not perform as effectively in an uncontrolled, real-world farming set-up as it does in the controlled experimental setting (Lu et al., 2021; Khalid et al., 2023).
In computational terms, implementation of a CNN-based system in real time is also challenging in terms of processing speed, memory requirement as well as hardware requirements. Performance models are usually modeled with large amounts of computational power both during training and inference. Although the paper applies optimization methods and takes into consideration hardware lean architectures, there exists the trade-off between model complexity and real-time responsiveness. The system can be inefficient with low-end hardware or in the field with low processing powers (Gajjar et al., 2021; Shafik et al., 2024).
Lastly, the system will identify diseases in the predefined classes that are contained in the dataset. The system is not guaranteed to classify novel or rare disease variants absent from the training dataset, so the system will be restricted in its ability to generalize. On-going retraining and updating of data is thus required to have accuracy as time goes by. Also, although the project proves solanaceous crops, it would need retraining to other crops and might require further preprocessing changes to explain the difference in leaf morphology and disease presentation (Ferentinos, 2018; Borhani et al., 2022).
In spite of these restrictions, the research piaper offers an effective model of real-time disease identification and shows how the CNN-based systems could be used to improve agricultural surveillance and decision-making. It is important to take these limitations into consideration to identify the extent of applicability and future enhancement of AI-based technological advancements in agriculture.
1.8 Definition of Operational Terms 
In order to have clarity and consistency in the current study, some technical and domain specific terms are enacted as below:
i. Convolutional Neural Network (CNN): This is a form of deep learning model tailored to handle data that has a grid-like form, e.g. images. CNNs can extract hierarchical features (edges, textures, and patterns) in input images automatically using convolutional layers, pooling layers and fully connected layers. In this paper, CNNs are applied to detect and determine the type of leaf disease in solanaceous crops (Ferantinos, 2018; Lu et al., 2021).
ii. Real-Time Detection: A system that is capable of taking input data and giving immediate output or a feedback without any noticeable delay. Within this paper, real-time detection means the collection of leaf images through a camera and the real-time classification of the images to be healthy or diseased using the trained CNN (Gajjar et al., 2021; Khalid et al., 2023).
iii. Leaf Disease: This is an illness that happens to the leaves of a plant, and is in most cases, brought about by pathogens (e.g. fungi, bacteria or viruses). The common symptoms are discoloration, spots, lesions and wilting. This study focuses on leaf diseases in solanaceous crops because such diseases are the most evident and the most diagnostic signs of plant health.
iv. Data Preprocessing: Data Preprocessing is a collection of methods that are applied to unstructured input data in order to enhance the effectiveness of machine learning models. Preprocessing I n this work involves the downsizing of images to standard sizes, pixel value normalization, augmentation, like image rotation, flipping, and scaling, to enhance the variety of data and enhance CNN generalization (Hassan et al., 2021; Shafik et al., 2024).
v. Transfer Learning: This is a deep learning technique in which a model that has been trained to pass a certain task is modified to perform a similar task. This is done to utilize the pre-trained CNN models to have better accuracy and shorter training time on the small plant leaf image sets (Borhani et al., 2022; Hassan et al., 2021).
vi. Precision, Recall, Accuracy, and F1-Score: These are standard evaluation measures that are employed to determine the model performance in all classification models. Accuracy is the ratio of right predictions, precision is the ratio of accurate positive predictions as compared to all positive predictions, recall is the capacity to identify all true positive cases, and F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall. These measurements are employed in the determination of the effectiveness of the CNN model in identifying leaf diseases correctly.
vii. Solanaceous Crops: The family of crops, which encompasses economically significant crops, i.e. tomato (Solanum lycopersicum), potato (Solanum tuberosum), pepper (Capsicum spp.), and eggplant (Solanum melongena). Such crops are very vulnerable to diseases of the leaves and are the target of the given research because of their economic and agricultural value.
viii. Augmentation: This is a data processing method that is applied to artificially boost the size and variety of a dataset by generating distorted versions of existing images. These techniques are rotations, flips, scaling, cropping and color adjustments. The process of augmentation can make CNN models more capable of generalization and prevent overfitting (Lu et al., 2021; Tugrul et al., 2022).
ix. Inference: The procedure through which a trained model comes up with predictions of unseen data that has not been utilized before. The term inference will be used in this study, where the CNN model will consider the image of the leaf and classify it as healthy or diseased as it performs its real-time functionality.
x. Pooling Layer: A layer in CNN that reduces the size of the image representation while retaining essential features, helping to reduce computation and prevent overfitting.
xi. Activation Function: A function in a neural network that decides whether a neuron should be activated or not. Examples include ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) and Softmax.
xii. Dataset: A collection of plant images (healthy and diseased) used to train, validate, and test the CNN model.
xiii. Pathogen: Any microorganism (bacteria, virus, fungus) that causes disease in plants.
xiv. Computer Vision: A field of artificial intelligence that allows computers to interpret and process visual data from the world, such as plant leaves in images.
xv. Overfitting: A problem in machine learning where the model performs well on training data but poorly on new, unseen data because it has memorized patterns rather than generalized them.

2.2 Conceptual Review 
2.2.1 Solanaceous Crops and Disease Challenges
Among the most commonly grown food crops in the world, solanaceous crops, such as tomato, potatoes, pepper and eggplant, are included. These are crops that are of great importance in terms of food security and agricultural economies. They are, however, very susceptible to diverse diseases incurred by fungi, bacteria and viruses. Early blight, late blight, bacterial wilt, and mosaic viruses are some of the diseases that cause severe losses of yields when they are not identified and managed at an early stage (Anim-Ayeko et al., 2023; Vasconez et al., 2024).
2.2.2 Plant Disease Detection Approaches
Conventional plant disease detection systems are based on the manual examination of the crops or by an agriculturalist to a large extent. Although effective in certain instances, the methods are subjective, time consuming, and not practical to use in large scale farming. Diagnostic methods that are based in the laboratory are more accurate, but expensive and cannot be used in the field in real-time. These constraints have encouraged the integration of the automated and intelligent disease detection systems (Ferentinos, 2018).
2.2.3 Image-Based Disease Detection 
Image-based disease detection is a task that requires taking a picture of plant leaves and interpreting visible symptoms namely color changes, lesions, and changes in the texture. Other previous methods relied on manual image processing and machine learning algorithms that relied on hand-crafted features. Even though these modalities delivered moderate success, their effectiveness was usually influenced by lighting and the background noise as well as feature selection constraints (Sladojevic et al., 2016).
2.2.4 Deep Learning in Plant Detection
Deep learning has also revolutionized the analysis of images in the sense that it allows the extraction of features automatically in the raw images, thus, doing away with the manual extraction of features. This is enabled by the ability of deep learning models to acquire complex and discriminative patterns with big datasets of images. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have become the most common type of the deep learning model to detect plant diseases because they are capable of focusing on both the spatial and hierarchical characteristics present in an image. CNN architectures have been developed to be sensitive to low-level features like edges and textures in lower layers and higher levels impart more complicated representations related to particular disease symptoms on the leaf surface. This renders them very applicable in agricultural purposes where conditions of obtaining images are not controlled. Experimental research has constantly demonstrated that CNN-based models are substantially more accurate at classifying and deliver superior generalization performance than the traditional machine learning techniques that use handcrafted features (Ferentinos, 2018; Jane et al., 2022). Consequently, CNNs are now used as a cornerstone to the current automated and real-time plant disease detection systems.
2.2.5 Real Time Disease Detection Systems
Real time plant disease detection systems are meant to deliver instantaneous diagnosis of plant health status due to image acquisition as images are taken in real time, usually using cameras, mobile phones, unmanned aerial vehicles or robotic platforms. The systems are meant to work with visual data in real-time and provide immediate feedback to farmers or other persons in the agricultural sector. The presence of real-time disease observation helps to make timely decisions and prevent the spread of diseases throughout the farm, as timely detection is provided, and accordingly, the application of the necessary treatment. This does not only reduce the cost of production but it also encourages the use of environmentally sustainable and precision agriculture. Nonetheless, introductions of real-time systems are associated with problems of computational efficiency and speed, as well as the capability of deploying the models in resource-constrained systems. Scholars have studied the industrialized CNN designs and deployment mechanisms that enable real-time performance via mobile and robotic systems, which proves the feasibility of such systems in the contemporary agricultural environment (Khalid et al., 2023; Hidayah et al., 2022).
2.3 Theoretical Framework
The study is based on the Deep Learning Theory and Pattern Recognition Theory that form the conceptual and theoretical background of how convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are trained to identify and classify plant disease patterns using images. The theories can be applied to automated plant diseases detection in particular, as they provide the mechanism of feature detection, hierarchical learning, and pattern recognition, which are key to the work of CNN-based diagnostics.
2.3.1 Deep Learning Theory
The idea underlying deep learning theory is that multi-layered neural networks have the ability to learn hierarchical representations of complex data automatically. When used in image analysis, CNNs utilize several layers of connected neurons to sequentially generate features of more complex feature of raw image data. The first convolutional layers are concerned with the low-level features (edges, corners and textures) that are the foundations of the more complex features. It is the intermediate layers that put these low-level features together to determine shapes, color variations and localized patterns that could be signs of disease. Lastly, the underlying layers are trained to create very abstract representations of a disease-specific visual signature of a certain plant and can make accurate predictions without manually feature-engineering them (Toda et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020).
Along with feature extraction, the deep learning theory focuses on the possibility of the neural networks to generalize training data to unknown examples. This is especially relevant in the detection of plant diseases, where the presence of differences in lighting, leaf posture, and background, and the severity of the disease may influence the quality of the image. Due to the hierarchical learning and weight sharing processes, CNNs are capable of learning strong features that retain diagnostic accuracy over a variety of conditions, which is useful to real-time applications in agriculture (Ferantinos, 2018; Jane et al., 2022).
2.3.2 Pattern Recognition Theory
Pattern recognition theory is concerned with identification and categorisation of patterns in data. Leaves have characteristic visual patterns when diseased in case of the detection of plant diseases. These patterns can be spots, lesions, discoloration or deformity which are diagnostic markers. Pattern recognition theory According to the theory, computational systems can also be trained in order to identify these patterns, categorize them, and to make informed decisions based on features they have been trained to recognize.
The CNNs are effective pattern recognition systems and they automatically learn discriminative features of plant images that are used to identify healthy or diseased states. CNNs are able to learn the useful features, unlike traditional pattern recognition algorithms, which use a handcrafted feature significantly, enhancing accuracy and scalability (Jung et al., 2023; Sladojek et al., 2016). The enjoyment of deep feature extraction and pattern recognition renders CNNs very useful in the process of recognizing complex patterns of diseases that might be insidious or hard to see using human inspection only.
2.3.3 Study Theoretical Model.
The framework used in this research study, which takes an input-process-output (IPO) model, is used to design the real-time disease detection system. The structure is designed in the following way:
Input: Solanaceous crop leaves are taken in the form of images using cameras or mobile gadgets. These pictures can change in terms of resolution, lighting and background and depicts the field conditions in reality.
Process: Preprocessing is done to the captured images to increase quality and minimize noise. CNNs subsequently carry out extraction and feature-based classification wherein the presence or the absence of disease is established on the basis of the acquired patterns. The model is trained on the basis of labeled data that consists of images of healthy and diseased leaf to render optimal recognition performance.
Output: The system provides real time report of the health of the plants, including the nature of the disease in case it occurs. This output helps in making timely intervention as well as informed decision-making to manage the disease.
This theoretical framework will make the proposed system both methodologically sound and practical. It combines both hierarchical learning functions of CNNs and pattern recognition concepts to offer autonomous, dependable, and real-time plant disease detector to overcome the obstacles of manual inspection and late diagnosis methods used in the cultivation of solanaceous crops.
2.4 Empirical Review 
A number of empirical studies have examined deep learning in detection of plant diseases. Sladojevic et al. (2016) showed that deep neural networks are effective in classification of leaves and high accuracy was reached in disease identification. Mohameth et al. (2020) used the dataset of plant diseases in PlantVillage to test deep learning models and ensured the high level of classification in controlled conditions. On the same note, Jane et al. (2022) pointed out the upward trend in the use of CNNs in the diagnosis of plant diseases.
Studies that have been conducted recently have been centered on solanaceous crops and real time application. Based on the findings, Anim-Ayeko et al. The framework proposed by Khalid et al. (2023) is the detection of real-time plant health that uses deep CNNs, which proves the possibility of using deep learning models to diagnose health instantly. Vasconez et al. (2024) specifically focused on the application of visual symptom classification of bacterial wilt disease in tomato plants by using deep learning.
Advanced architectures and transfer learning have also been noticed. Shafik et al. (2024) demonstrated that transfer learning enhances the performance of the model and lessens the training duration whereas Borhani et al. (2022) addressed the use of vision transformers in the process of automated plant disease classification. Tugrul et al. (2022), Liu et al. (2021), and Shoaib et al. (2023) also described in detail the current trends, issues, and perspectives of deep learning-based plant disease detection in a review study.
2.5 Chapter Summary
The literature review shows that deep learning models, specifically CNN models, have dramatically enhanced the accuracy, efficiency and scalability of plant disease detection systems. The empirical results indicate that the classification performance is always high with the use of large datasets, transfer learning methods and optimized CNN architecture. Research has demonstrated that deep learning models can identify complex patterns of diseases, process changes in leaf appearance, and can even offer automated decision support, which is a major improvement compared to the traditional methods of manual or machine learning-based (Ferentinos, 2018; Jane et al., 2022; Khalid et al., 2023).
Yet, there are a few limitations and obstacles that can be observed. Most research uses highly controlled datasets with homogeneous backgrounds and high quality images which do not represent the real field conditions of variability and unpredictability such as variations in lighting, occlusions, and noise in the environment. Moreover, certain CNN models are computationally demanding and therefore demand high processing power and memory that cannot be deployed to mobile or edge devices in real-time to detect diseases. The research on the real-time disease detection system specifically designed to apply to solanaceous crops is also understudied, especially in the context of real-life farming, where quick and accurate disease diagnosis can be more important (Hidayah et al., 2022; Shafik et al., 2024).
These gaps serve to highlight the importance of additional studies on the topic, and hence the current study that will be trying to design a strong, real-time CNN-based disease detection system in solanaceous crops. The offered system aims to provide a balance between high diagnostic accuracy and computational efficiency to guarantee that it can be applied in the field. Through filling these gaps, the study can improve the creation of smart agricultural systems, encourage precision farming, and become a contributor to sustainable crop management strategies capable of reducing losses in yields and improving food security (Vasconez et al., 2024; Anim-Ayeko et al., 2023).

Another technological aspect that has been discussed in the chapter is the detection systems of plant diseases in real-time, as they are likely to provide farmers with immediate feedback and allow them to take the necessary actions in the field (Gajjar et al., 2021; Khalid et al., 2023). Transfer learning and data augmentation were found to show great improvement in the model performance especially in cases where datasets are small or even imbalanced (Borhani et al., 2022; Toda et al., 2019). Comparison of various CNN methods also indicated that the selection of a model is based on the target crop, disease complexity, size of the dataset, and the deployment environment, and that there is no universal solution to automated plant diseases detection (Jane et al., 2022; Vasconez et al., 2024).
Finally, the discussed literature shows that CNN-based disease detection systems are precise, scalable, and efficient methods of controlling plant health in solanaceous crops. The experience of these researches is the basis of the methodology elaborated in Chapter 3 that will be used to select datasets, CNN models, preprocessing algorithms, and deployment plans to create a powerful, real-time disease detection system

3.1 Research Design 
This study adopts an experimental research design aimed at developing and evaluating a real-time plant disease detection system for solanaceous crops, specifically tomato and potato, using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The experimental design provides a systematic approach to assess the effectiveness, reliability, and practical applicability of the proposed system under controlled and semi-controlled conditions.
The design involves capturing a wide range of images of tomato and potato leaves under varying environmental conditions, including different lighting levels, angles, and backgrounds, to simulate realistic field scenarios. Supervised learning forms the core of the experimental methodology, where the CNN model is trained on a carefully labeled dataset of healthy and diseased leaf images. By providing explicit labels for each image, the model is able to learn distinguishing visual features, such as lesions, discoloration, and texture changes, associated with specific diseases.
The experimental approach also allows for iterative testing and fine-tuning of model parameters, including the number of convolutional layers, filter sizes, activation functions, learning rate, and batch size. This process ensures that the system achieves optimal performance in terms of classification accuracy, robustness to environmental variations, and computational efficiency. Additionally, the design incorporates multiple evaluation stages, including validation on unseen datasets and real-time testing on live leaf images, to measure the system's capability to provide timely and accurate disease diagnosis.
By combining controlled experiments with practical evaluation, the experimental research design not only verifies the technical performance of the CNN model but also examines its operational feasibility in real-world agricultural contexts. This methodology ensures that the study produces reliable, reproducible, and actionable results, providing a solid foundation for the development of an intelligent, real-time plant disease detection system suitable for deployment in modern precision agriculture.
3.2 Architectural Framework 
The architectural structure of the proposed real-time plant disease detection system gives a systematic illustration of the flow of data in the system, which involves acquisition of image, disease classification and the output. The framework is meant to make sure that the system is efficient, accurate and also in real-time even when the field conditions vary.
The system architecture will be structured in various connected levels:
i. Input Layer: This layer captures the images of the leaves of the solanaceous crops. This can be done through high-resolution cameras, mobile devices or robotic platforms to get images. The input layer is the interface with the data and its design is such that the images are taken with a quality that is good, where the analysis will be effective.
ii. Preprocessing Layer: Raw pictures are characterized with noise, bad light, and clutter in the background. The preprocessing layer normalizes the size of the image, standardizes the pixel values and removes noise using the noise reduction techniques. Also in this layer, data augmentation like rotation, flipping and brightness adjustment is done in order to increase the diversity of the datasets and the overall generalization power of the CNN model.
iii. Feature Extraction Layer: The CNN based feature extraction layer is the central part of the architecture. Convolutional layers spot and isolate locality features like edges, spots and textures. The pooling layers help to decrease the dimensions and maintain the essential features, and the subsequent layers of the CNN learn more abstract features that would be able to represent the peculiarities of various diseases of plants. The hierarchical learning allows the system to identify the differences between healthy and diseased leaves that are subtle.
iv. Classification Layer: The classification stage has fully connected layers that interpret the features extracted to define the disease category or a healthy status of the leaf. The result of this layer is the resultant predicted class label, produced with the help of an appropriate activation function, e.g. softmax, to create probabilities of each of the possible diseases.
v. Output Layer: The last layer shows the user the classification results on a real-time basis. Production of the identified type of disease (or healthy condition) can be furthered into prescribed measures, such as proposed treatment options or emergency intervention warnings.
This hierarchical architecture structure makes sure that the architecture is modular, scaleable and adaptable to real world implementation. The architecture enables effective data flow, proper disease detection and quick real-time response due to clearly defining the role of each layer, which is the foundation of the intelligent plant disease detection system.
3.3 Algorithms and Schematic Framework 
3.3.1 Algorithm Steps 
The proposed real-time plant disease detection system is based on a systematic algorithm to capture, process and classify the leaf images. The algorithm combines image processing, feature extraction with CNN, and classification into a stepwise workflow, making it accurate and efficient to work with the real-time functioning. The algorithm works in the following steps:
1. Data Acquisition: Take pictures of tomato and potato leaves with cameras, mobile phones or robots. Pictures are captured in both controlled and field conditions to depict real life situations.
2. Image Preprocessing: Rescale pictures to a standard size which can be used by CNN input layer. This is to normalize the pixel values so as to enhance the convergence of models during training. Noise cancelling methods would be used to reduce the background noise.
3. Data augmentation: (rotation, flipping, zooming, changing brightness) should be performed in order to diversify the data (to improve model generalization).
4. CNN Extraction of features:
i. Use convolutional layers in order to identify local features like edges, textures and spots.
ii. Apply pooling layers on the way to decrease the spaces but important characteristics are preserved.
iii. Use more CNN layers to acquire layers of disease patterns which are complex and hierarchical.
iv. Classification:
v. Extracted features: are analyzed by fully connected layers to give a prediction of the health status of the leaf.
vi. The softmax activation option should be used to provide probabilities of all possible diseases or healthy conditions.
vii. The final label of the classes should be determined on the basis of the most likely probability.
5. Output Generation:
i. Presentation of the classification outcome in real-time.
ii. Provide suggested actions or alerts optional to aid in disease management decision making.

3.4 Software Specifications
Designing the real-time plant disease detection system will be based on a set of programming languages, deep learning frameworks, and auxiliary software tools to facilitate the efficient training of models, their correct classification, and the uncomplicated application. The software specifications of the main tools and technologies that will be employed in this study include the following:
i. Programming Language:
The main programming language is Python, as it is flexible, simple and has broad support of machine learning and deep learning applications. Python has an extensive library or ecosystem of libraries and frameworks that are appropriate to image processing, model training, and deployment.
ii. Deep Learning Frameworks:
The analysis and design of CNN models are done using TensorFlow or PyTorch. These frameworks complement the capabilities of the CPU in terms of processing large image datasets through the use of a graphics card that will accelerate a neural network when the network is specified and enabled to harness deeper neural network functionalities, including automatic differentiation and pinpointing of the gradient location during network operations.
iii. Image Processing Libraries:
Image acquisition, preprocessing and augmentation are done using OpenCV, and Pillow (PIL). These libraries offer resizing, normalization, noise suppression and other transformation functions that may be required to prepare the images as inputs to CNN.
iv. Data Handling and Analysis:
NumPy and Pandas are applied to work with arrays, matrices and structured data, which can easily be integrated with the deep learning model.
v. Visualization Tools:
The training metrics, confusion matrices, and other graphic displays to assess the model performance are plotted with the help of Matplotlib and Seaborn.
vi. Development Environment:
The integrated development environment (IDE) includes Jupyter Notebook or PyCharm, where the models of the CNN are coded, tested, and experimented on repeatedly.
vii. Deployment Tools:
To be able to run the trained models on mobile devices or embedded systems in real-time, TensorFlow Lite or ONNX can be used to convert the trained models into lightweight formats that have been optimized to be fast and memory-efficient.
All of this software will allow creating a powerful framework of a real-time plant disease detection system, which allows creating an efficient model, classify diseases accurately, and apply it in practice to the field.
3.5 Hardware Specifications
The detecting system of the real-time plant diseases depends on the effective hardware and the efficient high-performance computational resources used to train the model and deploy it in the field. The hardware specifications that are used to build, train, and deploy the CNN-based system are as follows:
A. Processing Unit:
i. Central Processing Unit (CPU): A minimum of 4 cores on a multi-core CPU is suggested in case of simple preprocessing, data processing, and low load model processing.
ii. Graphics Processing Unit (GPU): A dedicated GPU with CUDA support is an ideal choice in the training of CNN models because it can be used to calculate things in parallel, greatly shortening the training time and improving the efficiency.
B. Memory (RAM):
At least 16 GB RAM is necessary to work with large image datasets, do data augmentation, and train deep learning models without interruptions and memory overflow.
Storage:
The combination of dataset, model weights, training logs and output results will require a storage of at least 500 GB. The storage of SSD should be increased with faster speeds to enhance the read/write speed of the data during training and preprocessing.
C. Image Capture Devices:
i. High-Resolution Camera: To obtain images of leaves with high level of clarity that can help in detecting small disease patterns.
ii. Mobile Devices: Field data collection may be done on Smartphones or tablet computers with high camera specifications.
iii. Robotic Platforms: Large-scale image acquisition in agricultural fields can be carried out by automated platforms that have cameras.
3.6 Evaluation Metrics 
To test the performance of the proposed system in recognizing dynamic human gestures, classifications are made employing standard metrics.
TP = True Positive
TN = True Negative
FP = False Positive
FN = False Negative
i. Accuracy
Accuracy is the number of accurately classified gesture samples divided by the total number of samples.
                                   Accuracy = (TP+TN)/(TP+TN+FP+FN)
Accuracy is widely used as a general measure of the overall performance of a gestural recognition study (Núñez et al., 2018).
ii.  Precision
Precision measures how well a method predicts positively classified gestures in relation to all positively predicted gestures. 
                                 Precision =  TP/(TP+FP)
iii. Recall
Recall or sensitivity measures the percentage of correctly identified positive gestures out of all actual positive gestures.
                                 Recall =  TP/(TP+FN)
iv. F1 Score
As known, the value of the F1-score is defined as the harmonic average of precision and recall.
                  F1 Score =  (2 × Precision ×Recall )/(Precision + Recall)
 Such metrics are widely used in deep learning-based gesture recognition systems to ensure a holistic evaluation of the performance of these systems (Goodfellow et al., 2016; Donahue et al., 2015).
